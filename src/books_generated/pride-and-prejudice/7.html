CHAPTER VII.

1Mr. Bennet’s property consisted almost entirely in an estate of two thousand a year, which, unfortunately for his daughters, was entailed, in default of heirs male, on a distant relation; and their mother’s fortune, though ample for her situation in life, could but ill supply the deficiency of his. 2Her father had been an attorney in Meryton, and had left her four thousand pounds.

3She had a sister married to a Mr. Philips, who had been a clerk to their father and succeeded him in the business, and a brother settled in London in a respectable line of trade.

4The village of Longbourn was only one mile from Meryton; a most convenient distance for the young ladies, who were usually tempted thither three or four times a week, to pay their duty to their aunt, and to a milliner’s shop just over the way. 5The two youngest of the family, Catherine and Lydia, were particularly frequent in these attentions: their minds were more vacant than their sisters’, and when nothing better offered, a walk to Meryton was necessary to amuse their morning hours and furnish conversation for the evening; and, however bare of news the country in general might be, they always contrived to learn some from their aunt. 6At present, indeed, they were well supplied both with news and happiness by the recent arrival of a militia regiment in the neighbourhood; it was to remain the whole winter, and Meryton was the head-quarters.

7Their visits to Mrs. Philips were now productive of the most interesting intelligence. 8Every day added something to their knowledge of the officers’ names and connections. 9Their lodgings were not long a secret, and at length they began to know the officers themselves. 10Mr. Philips visited them all, and this opened to his nieces a source of felicity unknown before. 11They could talk of nothing but officers; and Mr. Bingley’s large fortune, the mention of which gave animation to their mother, was worthless in their eyes when opposed to the regimentals of an ensign.

12After listening one morning to their effusions on this subject, Mr. Bennet coolly observed,—

13“From all that I can collect by your manner of talking, you must be two of the silliest girls in the country. 14I have suspected it some time, but I am now convinced.”

15Catherine was disconcerted, and made no answer; but Lydia, with perfect indifference, continued to express her admiration of Captain Carter, and her hope of seeing him in the course of the day, as he was going the next morning to London.

16“I am astonished, my dear,” said Mrs. Bennet, “that you should be so ready to think your own children silly. 17If I wished to think slightingly of anybody’s children, it should not be of my own, however.”

18“If my children are silly, I must hope to be always sensible of it.”

19“Yes; but as it happens, they are all of them very clever.”

20“This is the only point, I flatter myself, on which we do not agree. 21I had hoped that our sentiments coincided in every particular, but I must so far differ from you as to think our two youngest daughters uncommonly foolish.”

22“My dear Mr. Bennet, you must not expect such girls to have the sense of their father and mother. 23When they get to our age, I dare say they will not think about officers any more than we do. 24I remember the time when I liked a red coat myself very well—and, indeed, so I do still at my heart; and if a smart young colonel, with five or six thousand a year, should want one of my girls, I shall not say nay to him; and I thought Colonel Forster looked very becoming the other night at Sir William’s in his regimentals.”

25“Mamma,” cried Lydia, “my aunt says that Colonel Forster and Captain Carter do not go so often to Miss Watson’s as they did when they first came; she sees them now very often standing in Clarke’s library.”

26Mrs. Bennet was prevented replying by the entrance of the footman with a note for Miss Bennet; it came from Netherfield, and the servant waited for an answer. 27Mrs. Bennet’s eyes sparkled with pleasure, and she was eagerly calling out, while her daughter read,—

28“Well, Jane, who is it from? 29What is it about? 30What does he say? 31Well, Jane, make haste and tell us; make haste, my love.”

32“It is from Miss Bingley,” said Jane, and then read it aloud.

33“My dear friend,

34“If you are not so compassionate as to dine to-day with Louisa and me, we shall be in danger of hating each other for the rest of our lives; for a whole day’s tête-à-tête between two women can never end without a quarrel. 35Come as soon as you can on the receipt of this. 36My brother and the gentlemen are to dine with the officers. 37Yours ever,

38“Caroline Bingley.”

40“With the officers!” cried Lydia: “I wonder my aunt did not tell us of that.”

41“Dining out,” said Mrs. Bennet; “that is very unlucky.”

42“Can I have the carriage?” said Jane.

43“No, my dear, you had better go on horseback, because it seems likely to rain; and then you must stay all night.”

44“That would be a good scheme,” said Elizabeth, “if you were sure that they would not offer to send her home.”

45“Oh, but the gentlemen will have Mr. Bingley’s chaise to go to Meryton; and the Hursts have no horses to theirs.”

46“I had much rather go in the coach.”

47“But, my dear, your father cannot spare the horses, I am sure. 48They are wanted in the farm, Mr. Bennet, are not they?”

49“They are wanted in the farm much oftener than I can get them.”

50“But if you have got them to-day,” said Elizabeth, “my mother’s purpose will be answered.”

51She did at last extort from her father an acknowledgment that the horses were engaged; Jane was therefore obliged to go on horseback, and her mother attended her to the door with many cheerful prognostics of a bad day. 52Her hopes were answered; Jane had not been gone long before it rained hard. 53Her sisters were uneasy for her, but her mother was delighted. 54The rain continued the whole evening without intermission; Jane certainly could not come back.

55“This was a lucky idea of mine, indeed!” said Mrs. Bennet, more than once, as if the credit of making it rain were all her own. 56Till the next morning, however, she was not aware of all the felicity of her contrivance. 57Breakfast was scarcely over when a servant from Netherfield brought the following note for Elizabeth:—

58“My dearest Lizzie,

59“I find myself very unwell this morning, which, I suppose, is to be imputed to my getting wet through yesterday. 60My kind friends will not hear of my returning home till I am better. 61They insist also on my seeing Mr. Jones—therefore do not be alarmed if you should hear of his having been to me—and, excepting a sore throat and a headache, there is not much the matter with me.

62“Yours, etc.”

63“Well, my dear,” said Mr. Bennet, when Elizabeth had read the note aloud, “if your daughter should have a dangerous fit of illness—if she should die—it would be a comfort to know that it was all in pursuit of Mr. Bingley, and under your orders.”

64“Oh, I am not at all afraid of her dying. 65People do not die of little trifling colds. 66She will be taken good care of. 67As long as she stays there, it is all very well. 68I would go and see her if I could have the carriage.”

69Elizabeth, feeling really anxious, determined to go to her, though the carriage was not to be had: and as she was no horsewoman, walking was her only alternative. 70She declared her resolution.

71“How can you be so silly,” cried her mother, “as to think of such a thing, in all this dirt! 72You will not be fit to be seen when you get there.”

73“I shall be very fit to see Jane—which is all I want.”

74“Is this a hint to me, Lizzy,” said her father, “to send for the horses?”

75“No, indeed. 76I do not wish to avoid the walk. 77The distance is nothing, when one has a motive; only three miles. 78I shall be back by dinner.”

79“I admire the activity of your benevolence,” observed Mary, “but every impulse of feeling should be guided by reason; and, in my opinion, exertion should always be in proportion to what is required.”

80“We will go as far as Meryton with you,” said Catherine and Lydia. 81Elizabeth accepted their company, and the three young ladies set off together.

82“If we make haste,” said Lydia, as they walked along, “perhaps we may see something of Captain Carter, before he goes.”

83In Meryton they parted: the two youngest repaired to the lodgings of one of the officers’ wives, and Elizabeth continued her walk alone, crossing field after field at a quick pace, jumping over stiles and springing over puddles, with impatient activity, and finding herself at last within view of the house, with weary ancles, dirty stockings, and a face glowing with the warmth of exercise.

84She was shown into the breakfast parlour, where all but Jane were assembled, and where her appearance created a great deal of surprise. 85That she should have walked three miles so early in the day in such dirty weather, and by herself, was almost incredible to Mrs. Hurst and Miss Bingley; and Elizabeth was convinced that they held her in contempt for it. 86She was received, however, very politely by them; and in their brother’s manners there was something better than politeness—there was good-humour and kindness. 87Mr. Darcy said very little, and Mr. Hurst nothing at all. 88The former was divided between admiration of the brilliancy which exercise had given to her complexion and doubt as to the occasion’s justifying her coming so far alone. 89The latter was thinking only of his breakfast.

90Her inquiries after her sister were not very favourably answered. 91Miss Bennet had slept ill, and though up, was very feverish, and not well enough to leave her room. 92Elizabeth was glad to be taken to her immediately; and Jane, who had only been withheld by the fear of giving alarm or inconvenience, from expressing in her note how much she longed for such a visit, was delighted at her entrance. 93She was not equal, however, to much conversation; and when Miss Bingley left them together, could attempt little beside expressions of gratitude for the extraordinary kindness she was treated with. 94Elizabeth silently attended her.

95When breakfast was over, they were joined by the sisters; and Elizabeth began to like them herself, when she saw how much affection and solicitude they showed for Jane. 96The apothecary came; and having examined his patient, said, as might be supposed, that she had caught a violent cold, and that they must endeavour to get the better of it; advised her to return to bed, and promised her some draughts. 97The advice was followed readily, for the feverish symptoms increased, and her head ached acutely. 98Elizabeth did not quit her room for a moment, nor were the other ladies often absent; the gentlemen being out, they had in fact nothing to do elsewhere.

99When the clock struck three, Elizabeth felt that she must go, and very unwillingly said so. 100Miss Bingley offered her the carriage, and she only wanted a little pressing to accept it, when Jane testified such concern at parting with her that Miss Bingley was obliged to convert the offer of the chaise into an invitation to remain at Netherfield for the present. 101Elizabeth most thankfully consented, and a servant was despatched to Longbourn, to acquaint the family with her stay, and bring back a supply of clothes.
