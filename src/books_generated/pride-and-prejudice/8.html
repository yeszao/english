1CHAPTER VIII.

2AT five o’clock the two ladies retired to dress, and at half-past six Elizabeth was summoned to dinner. 3To the civil inquiries which then poured in, and amongst which she had the pleasure of distinguishing the much superior solicitude of Mr. Bingley, she could not make a very favourable answer. 4Jane was by no means better. 5The sisters, on hearing this, repeated three or four times how much they were grieved, how shocking it was to have a bad cold, and how excessively they disliked being ill themselves; and then thought no more of the matter: and their indifference towards Jane, when not immediately before them, restored Elizabeth to the enjoyment of all her original dislike.

6Their brother, indeed, was the only one of the party whom she could regard with any complacency. 7His anxiety for Jane was evident, and his attentions to herself most pleasing; and they prevented her feeling herself so much an intruder as she believed she was considered by the others. 8She had very little notice from any but him. 9Miss Bingley was engrossed by Mr. Darcy, her sister scarcely less so; and as for Mr. Hurst, by whom Elizabeth sat, he was an indolent man, who lived only to eat, drink, and play at cards, who, when he found her prefer a plain dish to a ragout, had nothing to say to her.

10When dinner was over, she returned directly to Jane, and Miss Bingley began abusing her as soon as she was out of the room. 11Her manners were pronounced to be very bad indeed,—a mixture of pride and impertinence: she had no conversation, no style, no taste, no beauty. 12Mrs. Hurst thought the same, and added,—

13“She has nothing, in short, to recommend her, but being an excellent walker. 14I shall never forget her appearance this morning. 15She really looked almost wild.”

16“She did indeed, Louisa. 17I could hardly keep my countenance. 18Very nonsensical to come at all! 19Why must she be scampering about the country, because her sister had a cold? 20Her hair so untidy, so blowzy!”

21“Yes, and her petticoat; I hope you saw her petticoat, six inches deep in mud, I am absolutely certain, and the gown which had been let down to hide it not doing its office.”

22“Your picture may be very exact, Louisa,” said Bingley; “but this was all lost upon me. 23I thought Miss Elizabeth Bennet looked remarkably well when she came into the room this morning. 24Her dirty petticoat quite escaped my notice.”

25“You observed it, Mr. Darcy, I am sure,” said Miss Bingley; “and I am inclined to think that you would not wish to see your sister make such an exhibition.”

26“Certainly not.”

27“To walk three miles, or four miles, or five miles, or whatever it is, above her ancles in dirt, and alone, quite alone! 28what could she mean by it? 29It seems to me to show an abominable sort of conceited independence, a most country-town indifference to decorum.”

30“It shows an affection for her sister that is very pleasing,” said Bingley.

31“I am afraid, Mr. Darcy,” observed Miss Bingley, in a half whisper, “that this adventure has rather affected your admiration of her fine eyes.”

32“Not at all,” he replied: “they were brightened by the exercise.” 33A short pause followed this speech, and Mrs. Hurst began again,—

34“I have an excessive regard for Jane Bennet,—she is really a very sweet girl,—and 35I wish with all my heart she were well settled. 36But with such a father and mother, and such low connections, I am afraid there is no chance of it.”

37“I think I have heard you say that their uncle is an attorney in Meryton?”

38“Yes; and they have another, who lives somewhere near Cheapside.”

39“That is capital,” added her sister; and they both laughed heartily.

40“If they had uncles enough to fill all Cheapside,” cried Bingley, “it would not make them one jot less agreeable.”

41“But it must very materially lessen their chance of marrying men of any consideration in the world,” replied Darcy.

42To this speech Bingley made no answer; but his sisters gave it their hearty assent, and indulged their mirth for some time at the expense of their dear friend’s vulgar relations.

43With a renewal of tenderness, however, they repaired to her room on leaving the dining-parlour, and sat with her till summoned to coffee. 44She was still very poorly, and Elizabeth would not quit her at all, till late in the evening, when she had the comfort of seeing her asleep, and when it appeared to her rather right than pleasant that she should go down stairs herself. 45On entering the drawing-room, she found the whole party at loo, and was immediately invited to join them; but suspecting them to be playing high, she declined it, and making her sister the excuse, said she would amuse herself, for the short time she could stay below, with a book. 46Mr. Hurst looked at her with astonishment.

47“Do you prefer reading to cards?” said he; “that is rather singular.”

48“Miss Eliza Bennet,” said Miss Bingley, “despises cards. 49She is a great reader, and has no pleasure in anything else.”

50“I deserve neither such praise nor such censure,” cried Elizabeth; “I am not a great reader, and I have pleasure in many things.”

51“In nursing your sister I am sure you have pleasure,” said Bingley; “and I hope it will soon be increased by seeing her quite well.”

52Elizabeth thanked him from her heart, and then walked towards a table where a few books were lying. 53He immediately offered to fetch her others; all that his library afforded.

54“And I wish my collection were larger for your benefit and my own credit; but I am an idle fellow; and though I have not many, I have more than I ever looked into.”

55Elizabeth assured him that she could suit herself perfectly with those in the room.

56“I am astonished,” said Miss Bingley, “that my father should have left so small a collection of books. 57What a delightful library you have at Pemberley, Mr. Darcy!”

58“It ought to be good,” he replied: “it has been the work of many generations.”

59“And then you have added so much to it yourself—you are always buying books.”

60“I cannot comprehend the neglect of a family library in such days as these.”

61“Neglect! 62I am sure you neglect nothing that can add to the beauties of that noble place. 63Charles, when you build your house, I wish it may be half as delightful as Pemberley.”

64“I wish it may.”

65“But I would really advise you to make your purchase in that neighbourhood, and take Pemberley for a kind of model. 66There is not a finer county in England than Derbyshire.”

67“With all my heart: I will buy Pemberley itself, if Darcy will sell it.”

68“I am talking of possibilities, Charles.”

69“Upon my word, Caroline, I should think it more possible to get Pemberley by purchase than by imitation.”

70Elizabeth was so much caught by what passed, as to leave her very little attention for her book; and, soon laying it wholly aside, she drew near the card-table, and stationed herself between Mr. Bingley and his eldest sister, to observe the game.

71“Is Miss Darcy much grown since the spring?” said Miss Bingley: “will she be as tall as I am?”

72“I think she will. 73She is now about Miss Elizabeth Bennet’s height, or rather taller.”

74“How I long to see her again! 75I never met with anybody who delighted me so much. 76Such a countenance, such manners, and so extremely accomplished for her age! 77Her performance on the pianoforte is exquisite.”

78“It is amazing to me,” said Bingley, “how young ladies can have patience to be so very accomplished as they all are.”

79“All young ladies accomplished! 80My dear Charles, what do you mean?”

81“Yes, all of them, I think. 82They all paint tables, cover screens, and net purses. 83I scarcely know any one who cannot do all this; and I am sure I never heard a young lady spoken of for the first time, without being informed that she was very accomplished.”

84“Your list of the common extent of accomplishments,” said Darcy, “has too much truth. 85The word is applied to many a woman who deserves it no otherwise than by netting a purse or covering a screen; but I am very far from agreeing with you in your estimation of ladies in general. 86I cannot boast of knowing more than half-a-dozen in the whole range of my acquaintance that are really accomplished.”

87“Nor I, I am sure,” said Miss Bingley.

88“Then,” observed Elizabeth, “you must comprehend a great deal in your idea of an accomplished woman.”

89“Yes; I do comprehend a great deal in it.”

90“Oh, certainly,” cried his faithful assistant, “no one can be really esteemed accomplished who does not greatly surpass what is usually met with. 91A woman must have a thorough knowledge of music, singing, drawing, dancing, and the modern languages, to deserve the word; and, besides all this, she must possess a certain something in her air and manner of walking, the tone of her voice, her address and expressions, or the word will be but half deserved.”

92“All this she must possess,” added Darcy; “and to all she must yet add something more substantial in the improvement of her mind by extensive reading.”

93“I am no longer surprised at your knowing only six accomplished women. 94I rather wonder now at your knowing any.”

95“Are you so severe upon your own sex as to doubt the possibility of all this?”

96“I never saw such a woman. 97I never saw such capacity, and taste, and application, and elegance, as you describe, united.”

98Mrs. Hurst and Miss Bingley both cried out against the injustice of her implied doubt, and were both protesting that they knew many women who answered this description, when Mr. Hurst called them to order, with bitter complaints of their inattention to what was going forward. 99As all conversation was thereby at an end, Elizabeth soon afterwards left the room.

100“Eliza Bennet,” said Miss Bingley, when the door was closed on her, “is one of those young ladies who seek to recommend themselves to the other sex by undervaluing their own; and with many men, I daresay, it succeeds; but, in my opinion, it is a paltry device, a very mean art.”

101“Undoubtedly,” replied Darcy, to whom this remark was chiefly addressed, “there is meanness in all the arts which ladies sometimes condescend to employ for captivation. 102Whatever bears affinity to cunning is despicable.”

103Miss Bingley was not so entirely satisfied with this reply as to continue the subject.

104Elizabeth joined them again only to say that her sister was worse, and that she could not leave her. 105Bingley urged Mr. Jones’s being sent for immediately; while his sisters, convinced that no country advice could be of any service, recommended an express to town for one of the most eminent physicians. 106This she would not hear of; but she was not so unwilling to comply with their brother’s proposal; and it was settled that Mr. Jones should be sent for early in the morning, if Miss Bennet were not decidedly better. 107Bingley was quite uncomfortable; his sisters declared that they were miserable. 108They solaced their wretchedness, however, by duets after supper; while he could find no better relief to his feelings than by giving his housekeeper directions that every possible attention might be paid to the sick lady and her sister.
